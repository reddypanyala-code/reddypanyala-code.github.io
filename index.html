About

From watching people use systems to designing systems for people.

I'm a senior Industrial Engineering student at the University of Wisconsin–Madison, graduating in 2026. My focus inside Industrial Engineering is Human Factors and Ergonomics — the part of the field that asks how people, tools, workplaces, and interfaces actually work together, not how we wish they did.

I got pulled into this work by a simple observation: most systems aren't broken because the technology fails. They're broken because the technology assumes the user has different hands, different eyes, different attention, or different patience than they actually have. That gap — between the system's design assumptions and a real person's body and mind — is where Human Factors lives, and where the most valuable problems are.

In ISyE 552 I learned to put numbers on that gap. Across three lab modules I measured posture with both observational scores and pose estimation, captured force and EMG to quantify muscular fatigue, and triangulated cognitive workload using NASA-TLX, performance, and HRV. Our team's semester project applied the same methods to VR training in automotive manufacturing. Every study taught me the same lesson differently — that one data source is rarely enough, and that the real finding is the one all of your sources agree on.

I want to apply this work in industries where small design issues have outsized consequences: medical devices, aviation, advanced manufacturing, and consumer products that serve users at the edges of variability. This portfolio is the case file: methods, findings, and the recommendations they support.

Final course project

Perceptions of VR Training in Manufacturing Environments

A team study evaluating whether virtual-reality engine-assembly training actually transfers to real manufacturing work — and where it introduces cognitive friction, cybersickness, or usability barriers.

The problem. VR training is being adopted across automotive manufacturing because it's cheaper and safer than physical setups. But adoption has stalled where users report cybersickness, where trainees can't tell whether they completed a step correctly, and where the simulation diverges from real tool feel. We asked: what does the workload, performance, and physiology data actually say about whether this works?

What we did. A pre-survey with manufacturing professionals (5 complete responses), then in-person testing with 10 engineering students completing a 24-task engine assembly/disassembly inside a Meta Quest VR program, instrumented with NASA-TLX, a cognitive-load inventory, the Simulator Sickness Questionnaire (SSQ), a presence survey, and Actiheart ECG.

What we found. Every participant asked at least one question — VR alone is not sufficient training. Cybersickness symptoms (8/10 reported at least one) correlated moderately with NASA-TLX (R² = 0.46). Prior VR experience didn't reduce workload as expected, but it did increase germane (learning-related) load — experienced users went deeper. The simulation scored low on realism and latency, high on adaptation speed.

We recommend layering psychomotor-feedback controllers, in-task question support, clearer instructional cues, and mandatory breaks at ~10-minute intervals.

  • 14.7 minMean completion time
  • R² = 0.46SSQ severity ↔ NASA-TLX

My role. I helped design the data-collection protocol, ran participant sessions, analyzed NASA-TLX and the cognitive-load inventory, scored the presence survey, drafted design recommendations, and wrote sections of the final report. Data triangulation

One source can mislead. Two can disagree. Three is where you find the truth.

Triangulation means using multiple data streams to cross-check a finding. Where they agree, that's the point of convergence — the actual problem. Below, the same logic applied across every lab and the final project.

Subjective

NASA-TLX, Borg RPE, SUS, interview responses, presence surveys.

Objective / Performance

Task time, accuracy, error rates, completion counts, learning curves.

Physiological

EMG (RMS, MPF), HRV (RMSSD, LF/HF), heart rate, pose-estimation angles.

Evidence-based design recommendations

Every recommendation traces back to the data that asked for it.

A small sample of the Finding → Evidence → Recommendation → Expected Impact pattern that runs through every lab and the project.

Finding (Lab 1)
Several participants spent meaningful time in elevated shoulder flexion during cargo handling.
Evidence
REBA shoulder-flexion correlation with score: r = 0.96. Maximum shoulder flexion reached ~80–90° in some trials; non-trivial time above the 45° threshold.
Recommendation
Stage materials at waist-to-chest height using a height-adjustable cart, turntable, or intermediate shelf so the shoulder doesn't repeatedly move into high flexion.
Expected impact
Reduced cumulative shoulder load over a shift; fewer forward-reach demands; lower musculoskeletal-injury risk.
Finding (Lab 2)
Endurance dropped sharply between 20% and 60% MVC and most participants underperformed Rohmert's idealized curve.
Evidence
Mean ET fell from ~140 s at 20% MVC to ~20 s at 60% MVC. RPE rose at ~3.3 units/min during sustained holds. EMG RMS rose and MPF fell — a textbook fatigue signature.
Recommendation
For tasks requiring sustained gripping above ~30% MVC, add micro-breaks, rotate task assignments, and switch to handles or power-assist tools that lower required grip force.
Expected impact
Slower onset of localized fatigue; fewer compensatory awkward postures; lower repetitive-strain risk.
Finding (Final project)
Every participant asked at least one question during the VR engine-assembly task — and Task 1 was disproportionately confusing (R² = 0.52 between Task-1 questions and overall completion time).
Evidence
An average 7.7 questions per trial; Task 1 generated 18 questions across the 10 participants and disproportionately predicted total time.
Recommendation
Add an in-task help layer (live operator, AI assistant, or contextual prompt) and rewrite the Task-1 instructions with clearer visual cues and a guided onboarding step.
Expected impact
Faster early-task ramp-up, fewer total interruptions, more learning-oriented (germane) cognitive load and less extraneous load.

Human Factors applied to this website

The portfolio itself is a human–system interface.

The user of this site is a professor, TA, recruiter, or industry reviewer. Their goal is to decide — usually within a minute or two — whether my work is interesting enough to keep reading. So the site is designed for that goal:

  • Visibility of system status. Sticky nav with an active-section indicator; clear page-heros on detail pages so you always know where you are.
  • Match with the real world. Plain-English headings; "Lab 1 — Postural Assessment" instead of an internal code name.
  • Aesthetic and minimalist design. One color accent (Iron-Man crimson + warm gold), one display family (Sora), one body family (Inter), and large amounts of whitespace.
  • Recognition rather than recall. Repeating card layout for labs, projects, and blogs so the reader doesn't relearn how to navigate.
  • Error prevention. No "under construction" sections. No broken links. The resume PDF and every detail page tested before publish.
  • Accessibility. Skip-link, semantic landmarks, focus-visible rings, alt text, color contrast, and a prefers-reduced-motion fallback that converts the helmet morph into a clean cross-fade.

The hero animation is an intentional design choice too: it gets attention without sacrificing readability for the visitors who don't want it (touch users get a tap toggle, reduced-motion users get a quiet cross-fade). That's the same trade-off a Human Factors designer makes when adding novelty to a real interface.
