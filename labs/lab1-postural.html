Lab 1 · Physical ergonomics · Feb 17, 2026

Postural Assessment — RULA/REBA vs. Pose Estimation

Five participants. Two manual-handling tasks. One question: when a quick observational score and a full-clip pose-estimation analysis disagree, which one tells the truth about ergonomic risk?

Purpose

RULA and REBA are quick observational tools that flag awkward postures and the likelihood of musculoskeletal strain. They're cheap and fast — but they score one selected moment, not the whole task. Camera-based pose estimation, by contrast, gives joint angles continuously across the clip. This lab tests both methods on the same trials so I could see what each catches that the other misses.

Methods

Five participants completed two short manual-handling tasks at a simple workstation built from a lightweight cardboard box and a table-height surface:

  • Task 1 — Packaging. A short reach/place/pack/reposition cycle, scored with RULA (upper-body strain).
  • Task 2 — Cargo handling. Lifting and transferring the box between two marked locations, scored with REBA (whole-body strain).

Each clip was paused at the posture that looked most demanding for that lab's target assessment, and I scored using the standard worksheets. The same trimmed videos were then run through the provided camera-based pose-estimation script to extract per-frame joint angles for trunk, neck, shoulder, elbow, wrist, and knee. From those angles I calculated mean / SD / max plus temporal exposure: % of time trunk flexion exceeded 20° and 60°, and % of time shoulder flexion exceeded 45°. Finally I correlated each observational score with the angle-based metrics across participants using Pearson's r.

What I found

Neck flexion stayed high across all of Task 1. Means ranged from 55.0° to 65.7°, consistent with the "head-down" posture of looking into a packing box. That's exactly why most RULA scores landed in the 3–4 range.

Trunk flexion was the biggest source of variability. P5 spent 57.1% of the Task-1 clip above 20° trunk flexion — sustained forward bending, not a quick peak — while P2 and P3 spent 0% there. Two participants can score similarly on RULA and have very different cumulative exposure.

Shoulder flexion was the strongest single predictor of both observational scores. Mean shoulder flexion correlated with RULA at r = 0.925 and with REBA at r = 0.958. This makes sense — both worksheets weight upper-arm posture heavily — and it's the cleanest case for using a pose-estimation pipeline as a screening tool before a manual scoring pass.

The methods complement each other. RULA/REBA translate one demanding moment into a standard action level. Pose estimation gives the full timeline of variability and exposure. Where the two disagreed, the most likely explanation was frame selection — a brief extreme posture inflated the RULA/REBA score even when the average posture across the clip was moderate.

Design recommendations

Bring the work to a more neutral posture
Adjust table height, use a tilt bin, or reposition the box so the worker doesn't have to look down and reach forward. Targets the consistently high neck flexion observed in Task 1.
Expected impact: lower mean neck flexion across the cycle and reduced sustained head-down time.
Stage cargo at waist-to-chest height
Use a height-adjustable cart, turntable, or intermediate shelf so the shoulder doesn't repeatedly move into high flexion. Several participants reached shoulder maxima near 80–90° during transfers, with non-trivial time above 45°.
Expected impact: reduced cumulative shoulder load over a shift; lower MSK-injury risk.
Add wrist-neutral handling aids
Better-shaped handles, a different grasp strategy, or gloves that improve coupling. Wrist deviation stayed high in both tasks.
Expected impact: lower distal-arm strain on repetitive cycles.

Reflection

The cleanest workflow uses both tools together. I'd start with pose estimation to screen the clip and automatically flag the candidate "worst" frames — say, the highest combined trunk and shoulder flexion — and then run RULA/REBA on those flagged frames to capture factors the camera can't infer (load, coupling, task context). When the two disagreed, that disagreement was usually the most informative finding: it told me that either the chosen frame wasn't representative or the pose model lost track of a joint.

This lab also reset my intuitions about how much body variability matters. P5 wasn't doing anything wrong — they just bend differently than the others — and a workstation that works for P1 may fail P5. Designing for the population means designing for that variance, not for an idealized average worker.
