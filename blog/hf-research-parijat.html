Guest Lecture 2 · Dr. Prakriti Parijat · Instacart

Turning Real Users Into Better Products

Dr. Parijat's talk on HF research in tech industry revealed a gap most coursework doesn't prepare you for: knowing how much evidence is enough to act on.

Context

Dr. Prakriti Parijat is a Human Factors researcher at Instacart, working at the intersection of user research, product design, and data science. Her career path moved from academic HF (where she trained) into tech industry product development — a transition that involves not just changing employers but fundamentally rethinking how you scope, justify, and communicate research findings. Her lecture addressed that transition directly, drawing on real examples from e-commerce and consumer technology.

Key Insight

The insight that crystallized most clearly for me was Dr. Parijat's distinction between what users say they do and what they actually do. Survey responses and interview answers are shaped by social desirability, recall bias, and the desire to appear reasonable. Behavioral data — what users actually click, how long they spend on a screen, where they abandon a flow — is less articulate but much harder to rationalize away. The most rigorous HF research in industry triangulates between the two: you use the behavioral data to identify where the gap is, and then you use qualitative methods to understand why the gap exists.

She gave the example of a checkout flow where a large percentage of users reached the payment screen and then abandoned the cart. Survey data suggested users found the interface "confusing." But observational data showed that users were not confused — they were price-checking. The mental model wasn't wrong; the users were doing exactly what rational shoppers do. The design implication was completely different once you understood the actual behavior rather than the stated one. Redesigning for "clarity" would have missed the real driver entirely.

Connection to Course Concepts

Dr. Parijat's framing maps directly onto the triangulation methodology we've practiced in ISyE 552 — the idea that no single data source gives you the whole picture, and that the most defensible conclusions emerge where multiple methods converge. In the lab context, that means layering self-report (NASA-TLX, Borg RPE), performance measures (accuracy, endurance time), and physiological signals (EMG, HRV) to triangulate the actual experience. In industry, the equivalent is layering surveys, behavioral analytics, and qualitative interviews.

The discussion of AI's impact on problem definition was also connected to course themes. As AI tools can increasingly automate the analysis layer — identifying patterns in behavioral data that would take a human analyst days — the HF researcher's scarce resource shifts from data processing to problem framing. Knowing which question to ask, and which evidence would actually change a product decision, becomes the high-value skill. That framing aligns with the emphasis throughout ISyE 552 on evidence-based recommendations: the technical analysis is the foundation, but the recommendation is where the value is created.

Personal Takeaway

Dr. Parijat's advice about "minimum amount of evidence" was counterintuitive but practical. Academic training tends to push toward thorough, comprehensive studies — large n, multiple conditions, statistical significance. Industry often requires a decision in two weeks based on whatever you can collect in one. Learning to work with thin data — and being explicit about what you can and can't conclude from it — is a skill that graduate seminars don't always teach directly.

Storytelling for stakeholder buy-in was another point that resonated. The finding that user behavior diverges from stated preference is only useful if you can communicate it in a way that changes what a PM or engineer decides to build. Translating a correlation coefficient or a task completion rate into a narrative that a non-technical audience can act on is a form of communication design — and it's as important as the analysis itself. I want to be more intentional about practicing that translation throughout my remaining coursework.

Closing Question

Dr. Parijat described the challenge of getting product teams to act on HF findings. In my experience from a student project perspective, the bottleneck is usually framing — researchers present findings, but don't connect them clearly to a product decision. Is there a format or workflow that works consistently for getting HF evidence into design decisions, or is it always context-dependent?
